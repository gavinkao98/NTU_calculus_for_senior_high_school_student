\section{The Precise Definition of a Limit}

In the previous sections, we used graphs, tables, and algebraic techniques to study limits. However, expressions such as ``\(x\) is close to \(a\)'' and ``\(f(x)\) is close to \(L\)'' are not precise enough for a rigorous definition. We now give the precise definition of a limit.

To motivate this definition, consider the function
\[
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
2x-1, & x\ne 3,\\[4pt]
6, & x=3.
\end{cases}
\]
When \(x\) is close to \(3\), but \(x\ne 3\), the value of \(f(x)=2x-1\) is close to \(5\). Thus it is natural to expect that
\[
\lim_{x\to 3}f(x)=5.
\]

\begin{example}
For the function
\[
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
2x-1, & x\ne 3,\\[4pt]
6, & x=3,
\end{cases}
\]
how close must \(x\) be to \(3\) in order to guarantee that \(f(x)\) differs from \(5\) by less than \(0.1\)?
\end{example}

\begin{solution}
We want
\[
|f(x)-5|<0.1.
\]
Since \(f(x)=2x-1\) for \(x\ne 3\), we have
\[
|f(x)-5|
=
|(2x-1)-5|
=
|2x-6|
=
2|x-3|.
\]
Therefore, to ensure that
\[
|f(x)-5|<0.1,
\]
it is enough to require that
\[
2|x-3|<0.1.
\]
This gives
\[
|x-3|<0.05.
\]
So if \(x\) is within \(0.05\) of \(3\), but \(x\ne 3\), then \(f(x)\) is within \(0.1\) of \(5\).

More generally, if we want
\[
|f(x)-5|<\varepsilon
\]
for some \(\varepsilon>0\), then it is enough to require that
\[
2|x-3|<\varepsilon,
\]
that is,
\[
|x-3|<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
\]
Thus for this example we may choose
\[
\delta=\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
\]
\end{solution}

The previous example shows the basic idea. We want to control the distance between \(f(x)\) and the limit \(L\) by controlling the distance between \(x\) and the point \(a\).

\begin{definition}
Let \(f\) be a function defined on an open interval containing \(a\), except possibly at \(a\) itself. We say that
\[
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L
\]
if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists a number \(\delta>0\) such that
\[
|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon
\qquad \text{whenever} \qquad
0<|x-a|<\delta.
\]
\end{definition}

Figure~\ref{fig:precise-limit} illustrates the definition. If \(x\) is restricted to lie in the interval
\[
(a-\delta,a+\delta),
\qquad x\ne a,
\]
then the corresponding values of \(f(x)\) are forced to lie in the interval
\[
(L-\varepsilon,L+\varepsilon).
\]
Notice that the value \(f(a)\) itself may be different from \(L\). The limit depends only on the behavior of \(f(x)\) when \(x\) is close to \(a\), not necessarily on the value of the function at \(x=a\).

\begin{remark}
The condition
\[
0<|x-a|<\delta
\]
means that \(x\) is within a distance \(\delta\) of \(a\), but \(x\ne a\).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The inequality
\[
|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon
\]
means that \(f(x)\) is within a distance \(\varepsilon\) of \(L\).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The statement
\[
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L
\]
must hold for every \(\varepsilon>0\), no matter how small \(\varepsilon\) is.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The definition can also be written in interval notation: for every \(\varepsilon>0\), there exists \(\delta>0\) such that if
\[
x\in (a-\delta,a+\delta)
\qquad \text{and} \qquad x\ne a,
\]
then
\[
f(x)\in (L-\varepsilon,L+\varepsilon).
\]
\end{remark}
